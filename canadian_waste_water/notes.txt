One conclusion from the analysis so far is that the
AREA of the waves is not decreasing in general. The
2024 wave was in most cases as big or bigger than
the 2023 and 2025 waves.

Also: there is some actual seasonality in these data!

Three years isn't really enough to say, but there does
appear to be a lull in the spring and a peak in the late
fall. This is quite different from the early years of the
ongoing pandemic.

Expanded cases to cover provinces and the whole country.

One possible pattern is that the higher the previous wave,
the lower (in area) the current wave, suggesting some

NOTE the unit of data is "copies/ml", so areas 
are days*copies/ml

Plausibly there is a summer wave followed by a winter
wave, with a minor peak around September 1 and a
major peak in the first few months of the year. It really
is too soon to say, and my goal here is NOT to model
the yearly curve with the greatest accuracy but to show
how an approximate model of the pandemic responds
to a particular intervention: masking or otherwise reduced
spread in the LULLs as opposed to at the PEAKs. That is,
intervening when cases are low, like an engineer, not
when cases are high, like a physician or MPH.

SO: the next step is to see if I can model all three waves
with a SEIRS model or MC across jurisdictions with
similar model parameters, ideally independent of the
size of the place. I'm interested in the smaller cities as
places that might experience extinction events, especially
in the spring.
